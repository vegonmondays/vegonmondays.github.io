The curious case of spoiled youngest daughters

Somehow, youngest daughters bring it out in us — a strange urge to strangle them yet massively pamper them. They're spoiled, oftentimes downright dumb, insanely entitled and yet coddled without a second thought. The days youngest daughters are missing at the table, the conversations are very adult, and very boring. The topics are — politics backed by well-informed facts and inside jokes only seasoned insiders know about; family matters — discussing heavy issues like illnesses, the strange dynamics of family relationships; money and discussions about work, about liabilities, about new ventures that need to be started and for which funding needs to be arranged.

During these times, there are no funny retorts, no unhinged comments, no fun poked at strange names / terms and no puns made of serious and big words. There are lesser smiles, lesser laughs, more sighs and heavier hearts. People get up from these conversations with slightly harder hearts, more wear and tear chipping away at the morale they had to grow at a young age that they will lose at 40, more chinks on the forced upon armour of adulthood that everyone except the youngest daughter (and especially, the youngest daughter) is forced to don.

I miss the youngest daughters. I miss the youngest daughter I was, a year, two years, a day ago. Sometimes for a minute or two or ten I am one — sometimes hugging the father and brother against their will, sometimes choosing to doze off on the man's shoulder while traveling or during a movie, sometimes resorting to baby talk with the dog while he licks my face.

She's often intensely annoying. Pushing all her family members to the limit just because she wants something done a certain way. To an untrained, responsible, mature outsider, this looks like the textbook definition of a spoiled child. Why can't she help more, get up more, read more, be more mature? To the untrained eye, it looks like she's doing nothing but demanding.

But to the family (they don't know it yet), she's doing so much more. She's fighting for their innocence by tightly holding on to hers, she's bringing the colours into their sepia-hued life, a hue that is boring and mature and goes with everything and adulthood. She's forcing laughs out of them, at her own expense, because their laughs were forced out of them by their lives and its trials. She's making them smile, making them question themselves for spoiling her so much, making them shake their heads in mock dismay, making them spell-bound with her adorned stupidity and thoughtful carelessness. She's making them feel, because that is what youngest daughters do.
